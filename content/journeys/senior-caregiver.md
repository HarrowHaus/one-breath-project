# Senior / caregiver journey

## Lead (myth correction first — this is the whole unlock)
You cannot smell carbon monoxide. You cannot see it or taste it. Roughly three out of four older Americans believe they would notice it in time. They are wrong — and that belief is exactly what makes this gas so good at killing the people who trust their own senses. The only thing that notices carbon monoxide is a working alarm.

## Why older adults are more at risk
Carbon monoxide is harder on older bodies, and its early signs — headache, dizziness, weakness, confusion — are easy to mistake for age, illness, or the flu. People often fall asleep before they realize anything is wrong.

## The one thing that gives it away
If you feel sick at home but better when you leave the house, and worse when you come back — treat that as a warning. That pattern is one of the few clues carbon monoxide gives you.

## Ten-minute checklist (for you or the person you care for)
Print this, or do it together on the phone.
1. Is there a carbon monoxide alarm on every level, near where people sleep? If not, that's the first buy.
2. Press the test button. Does it sound? If not, replace the batteries — or the alarm.
3. How old is the alarm? Most need replacing every 5–7 years. Check the date on the back.
4. Has the furnace or gas heater been checked by a professional in the last year?
5. Save two numbers in the phone: 911, and Poison Control at 1-800-222-1222.

## For caregivers
If you're helping a parent from a distance, you can order an alarm to their door, book their furnace inspection, and walk the checklist with them by phone. Ten minutes on the phone tonight is the whole job.
