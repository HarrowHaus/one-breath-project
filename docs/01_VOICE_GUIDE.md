# 01 — Voice Guide

**Who is speaking:** a civic-journalism newsroom that also advocates — a local public-service investigative desk that has decided this preventable death toll is unacceptable and is going to do something about it. Authoritative, sourced, human, morally clear. Not neutral-to-a-fault, not a brochure.

**What it is not:** the AI-assistant register. Strip every AI tell.

## Hard do / don't

**Kill these (AI tells):**
- Throat-clearing: "It's important to note," "It's worth mentioning," "In today's world."
- Hedging stacks: "may potentially," "could possibly," "in some cases might."
- Symmetrical mush: "on one hand… on the other hand…" that lands nowhere.
- Over-qualification that drains every sentence of force.
- Bullet-spam where prose should carry the reader.
- Passive voice / abstraction ("exposure events occurred") where a person and a verb belong ("she breathed it in her sleep").
- Windup conclusions: "In conclusion," "Ultimately, it is clear that."

**Keep these (the journalism):**
- Declarative sentences. Say the thing.
- Concrete nouns and real people. One furnace, one family, one night.
- Sources stated plainly, in the sentence: "the CDC," "the fire marshal's office," "at least."
- Honest floors: "at least 400 — and almost certainly more." Reporting, not hedging. Keep it.
- Moral clarity about preventable harm, without lecturing.
- The turn to agency: every hard truth is followed, close by, with a thing the reader can do.
- Rhythm: short sentences hit; longer ones explain. Vary them.

**Test for any sentence:** would it survive on the front of a respected local paper *and* move a scared reader toward one clear action? If it reads like a chatbot or a compliance memo, rewrite it.

**Honesty line:** sourcing and honest caveats are what *make* this a civic-journalism voice. Drop the AI hedging; never drop the reporting. Every number is Measured or Modeled and every claim is traceable.

**Hero / lede — the face before the number**
> Calvin Witherspoon Jr. went to sleep in his apartment and never woke up. So did his neighbor, Derrick Roper. It was January 2019, in public housing in Columbia, South Carolina. Investigators found what they'd find in far too many of these homes: not one carbon monoxide alarm in the entire complex. Weeks later, Witherspoon's daughter still caught herself dialing her father's number in the mornings, only to reach his voicemail.
>
> Carbon monoxide has no color, no smell, no taste. The people who died that night had no reason to be afraid — there was nothing to smell, and nothing to warn them. That is how this gas kills: quietly, in your sleep, in a home you trusted. And almost every death like it could have been stopped by a $20 alarm and an inspection that never happened.

**The small-numbers reframe — as published**
> Only a few hundred Americans die this way each year. That sounds small — until you learn that almost every death was preventable, that at least [20,000] more end up in emergency rooms, and that countless others are poisoned and sent home with a flu diagnosis. This is not a rare tragedy. It is a common, invisible one that we have simply agreed not to count.

**Senior myth-buster — correct the false belief, don't scare**
> You cannot smell carbon monoxide. You cannot see it or taste it. Roughly three out of four older Americans believe they would notice it in time. They are wrong — and that belief is exactly what makes this gas so good at killing the people who trust their own senses. The only thing that notices carbon monoxide is a working alarm.

**Renter — restore agency**
> You can't rewire your building. You can't replace the furnace. But you are not powerless. You have the right to ask, in writing, and you have the right to an answer. Here is exactly what to send your landlord — copy it, add your address, and hit send.

**Landlord — defensibility, not fear**
> After a poisoning, the law asks one question: did the owner do what a reasonable owner would do? Everything else follows from your answer. Here is what "yes" looks like, and how to be able to say it before anyone has to ask.

**Data explorer — auto-written sentence**
> In [Winnebago County] in [2023], at least [12] people were rushed to an emergency room for accidental carbon monoxide poisoning. Almost none of it had to happen.

**Resource finder — emergency first, always**
> If you think you're being poisoned right now, get outside into fresh air and call 911. Don't wait to feel sure. For everything else, here's who to contact near you.

**Closing call to action — agency, not guilt**
> This is one of the few dangers you can shut out of your home completely. A working alarm. A furnace someone actually checks. Ten minutes and a plan. That's the whole price of never becoming one of these stories.
